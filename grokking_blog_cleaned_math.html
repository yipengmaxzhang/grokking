Background

What Is Grokking?

In 2022, Power et al. discovered a striking phenomenon they called grokking: neural networks trained on small algorithmic datasets would first perfectly memorise the training set — achieving near-zero training loss while test loss remained high — and then, after many thousands of additional epochs, would abruptly generalise, with test loss collapsing to near zero almost overnight.

This notebook study reproduces and extends those findings using a 1-layer Transformer trained on the modular addition task: given inputs \(a\) and \(b\), predict \(a + b \pmod{97}\). The prime modulus \(P = 97\) ensures a rich structure in the cyclic group, giving the network something genuinely algorithmic to learn.

🎯

Core question: When a model appears to have "learned" a task (training loss ≈ 0), has it actually discovered the true algorithm — or is it merely memorising lookup tables? Grokking forces this question into sharp relief.

Experimental Setup

The model is a 1-layer Transformer with 4 attention heads, \(d_{\text{model}} = 128\), \(d_{\text{mlp}} = 512\), trained for up to 15,000 epochs with Adam (lr = 1e-3) and strong weight decay (λ = 1.0). 60% of all \((a, b)\) pairs are used for training; the remaining 40% are held out as a test set.

Checkpoints are saved every 500 epochs, enabling post-hoc mechanistic analysis at each training stage.

The Phenomenon

Three Phases of Training

Rather than a smooth monotonic improvement, grokking unfolds in three distinct phases, each with its own characteristic dynamics in both the loss curves and the weight norms.

I

Memorisation

Training loss collapses rapidly. Test loss stays high. The network is learning a lookup table, not an algorithm. Weight norms grow.

II

Circuit Formation

Loss curves plateau — nothing seems to be happening. But inside, the network is slowly forming a Fourier-based generalisation circuit. Weight decay quietly prunes noise.

III

Generalisation (Grokking!)

Test loss suddenly collapses. The memorisation lookup table has been replaced by a compact, weight-decay-regularised algorithm that generalises perfectly.

Grokking only occurs when the model is under sufficient regularisation pressure (weight decay). Without it, the memorisation solution is never displaced — the network never "bothers" to find the more efficient generalising circuit.

Reading the Loss Curves

In the log-scale view, the training loss (labelled "Restricted Loss") achieves near-zero values within the first ~1,000 epochs. The test loss ("Excluded Loss") stays near its initial value for thousands more epochs, giving the impression that nothing is happening. Then, around epochs 9,000–11,000, the test loss collapses — often within a few hundred epochs.

💡

Why does the weight norm decrease during grokking? The memorisation solution requires large weights (many look-up table entries). The generalising Fourier circuit is far more compact. Weight decay (L2 regularisation) continuously penalises large weights, eventually making the compact circuit more cost-effective — triggering the phase transition.

Mechanistic Interpretability: The Fourier Spectrum

To verify that the network discovered a Fourier-based algorithm (rather than just memorising), we apply a 1D Discrete Fourier Transform to the embedding matrix \(W_E\) (shape: 98 tokens × 128 dimensions). The Fourier power spectrum reveals which frequencies carry the model's information.

Figure 2 · grokking.ipynb · Cell 16

The embedding matrix's Fourier spectrum is strikingly sparse. Rather than distributing information evenly across all 49 possible frequencies, the model concentrates almost all variance in a small set of key frequencies — \(k \in \{14, 35, 41, 42, 52\}\). This sparsity is the smoking gun for a Fourier-based generalisation algorithm.

Why Fourier Sparsity Matters

If the network had merely memorised a lookup table, the embedding weights would have no particular structure — the Fourier spectrum would be flat or noisy. The fact that only 5 frequencies dominate (out of 49 possible) is evidence that the network has discovered a compact, structured algorithm.

These same key frequencies appear coherently in the attention weights, the MLP neuron activations, and the logit outputs — confirming a unified mechanistic circuit, not a coincidence.

Key frequencies: k ∈ { 14, 35, 41, 42, 52 }

Each frequency k contributes a sinusoidal embedding:
W_E[x, :] ≈ α·cos(2πkx/P) + β·sin(2πkx/P) for x = 0, …, 96
🔍

Connection to Figure 3a (Analysis notebook): The norm of each Fourier component of \(W_E\) shows the same sparsity pattern. Of the six non-zero frequencies detected, five (\(k \in \{14, 35, 41, 42, 52\}\)) appear consistently throughout the network — in both the embedding and the neuron-logit map \(W_L\).

Experiment 2: Epochs Until Generalisation vs. Data Fraction

A key prediction of the grokking story is that the phenomenon should be most pronounced at intermediate data fractions — too little data and the model can't learn at all; too much and it generalises easily without a memorisation phase. We sweep training fractions from 20% to 80% to test this.

Figure 3 · grokking.ipynb · Cell 18

Epochs required to reach test loss below 0.05 as a function of training data fraction. The relationship is highly non-linear: reducing the data fraction dramatically extends the grokking delay, suggesting there is a Goldilocks region for the phenomenon. With only 20% training data, generalisation requires orders of magnitude more training than with 80%.

Interpretation

The data fraction controls the pressure on the memorisation solution. A larger training set means more "facts" to memorise — the weight budget required for brute-force memorisation grows, while the compact Fourier circuit remains the same size. At some critical fraction, weight decay makes memorisation more expensive than generalisation, triggering the phase transition.

Grokking therefore emerges from a competition between two solutions: a memorising solution (cheap to find, expensive to maintain) and a generalising circuit (expensive to find, cheap to maintain).

The 2D Fourier transform of the logits, showing the norm of each frequency component. There are exactly 20 significant components, corresponding to the 5 key frequencies (\(k \in \{14, 35, 41, 42, 52\}\)) in both the sine and cosine direction — the precise signature of the \(\cos(k(a+b))\) algorithm.

📐

Why 20 components? Each key frequency \(k\) contributes four Fourier components: \(\cos(k \cdot a)\), \(\sin(k \cdot a)\), \(\cos(k \cdot b)\), \(\sin(k \cdot b)\). Together these implement \(\cos(k(a+b)) = \cos(ka)\cos(kb) - \sin(ka)\sin(kb)\), which via constructive interference gives the correct answer \(c = a + b \pmod{97}\).

The Periodicity Signature

The periodicity in Figure 4 is not a curiosity — it is a causal proof that the network is using trigonometric identities to compute modular addition. A lookup table would show no periodic structure; only a network that has internalised \(\sin\) and \(\cos\) functions would produce these clean sinusoidal activation maps.

Figure 5a — Neurons as Quadratic Fourier Forms

Figure 5a quantifies how well each of the 512 MLP neurons can be explained as a quadratic form on sinusoidal functions of the inputs. The claim is that each neuron essentially computes:

neuron_i(a, b) ≈ cos(w_k · (a + b) + φ_i)

where w_k = 2πk/P for some key frequency k, and φ_i is a neuron-specific phase shift.
📈
Figure 5a · Fraction of Variance Explained by Neuron (Histogram)

A histogram of the fraction of each neuron's variance explained by the best-fit quadratic sinusoidal form. The vast majority of neurons cluster near fraction = 1.0 — meaning they are almost perfectly described by a single sinusoidal frequency. This is remarkable: out of 512 neurons, almost all have specialised to be pure Fourier feature detectors.

What the Histogram Reveals

A vanilla MLP trained with SGD would be expected to have neurons with diverse, hard-to-interpret activation patterns. The fact that nearly all neurons in this grokked model are well-approximated by \(\cos(w_k(a+b) + \phi)\) is mechanistic evidence of an extremely clean internal algorithm.

Each neuron is essentially a cosine detector tuned to one of the five key frequencies. The downstream logit computation then aggregates these detectors to reconstruct the full modular addition answer via inverse Fourier transform.

🎵

Frequency Specialisation

Each neuron locks onto one key frequency (14, 35, 41, 42, or 52). Neurons of the same frequency form a "column" that collectively represents that Fourier component.

🔄

Phase Diversity

Within a frequency group, neurons differ in phase \(\phi_i\). Together they can represent both \(\sin(w_k(a+b))\) and \(\cos(w_k(a+b))\) — the full complex Fourier component.

✂️

Cleanup via Weight Decay

Neurons that don't cleanly fit a Fourier form are progressively suppressed by weight decay during the circuit formation phase — the network prunes its own noise.

Mechanistic Interpretability

The Learned Algorithm, Decoded

Combining all the mechanistic evidence — Fourier sparsity, periodic attention patterns, neuron specialisation — we can now state precisely what algorithm the network discovered.

Modular Addition via Trigonometric Identities

  1. Embedding: The embedding layer maps each token \(x\) to a vector containing \(\sin(w_k x)\) and \(\cos(w_k x)\) for each key frequency \(k\). This places the discrete modular arithmetic problem onto a continuous circle.

  2. Attention: The attention mechanism computes bilinear interactions between the embeddings of \(a\) and \(b\). Using the angle-sum identity: \(\cos(w_k(a+b)) = \cos(w_k a)\cos(w_k b) - \sin(w_k a)\sin(w_k b)\), the attention output contains the Fourier representation of \(a+b\).

  3. MLP: The MLP neurons (each a pure cosine detector) amplify the relevant Fourier components and suppress irrelevant ones. Each neuron responds maximally when \(a + b \pmod{97}\) passes through its phase.

  4. Unembedding: The unembedding matrix \(W_U\) maps the Fourier representation of \(a+b\) back to logits over \(\{0, \ldots, 96\}\). The correct answer \(c = a+b \pmod{97}\) gets constructive interference from all five frequency channels simultaneously, making it the highest-logit output.

logit(c | a, b) ∝ Σ_k α_k · cos(w_k · (a + b − c))

Maximised when c = a + b (mod 97), since all cosine terms = cos(0) = 1.

Extension: Modular Multiplication

For multiplication \(z = xy \pmod P\), the network exploits the cyclic structure of the multiplicative group. Since \(P = 97\) is prime, every non-zero element can be written as \(g^n \pmod{97}\) for a primitive root \(g\). Multiplication then reduces to addition in the exponent:

x · y ≡ g^(log_g(x) + log_g(y)) (mod P)

⟹ The network first computes discrete logarithms, then runs the same Fourier addition circuit on the logs.
Broader Implications

Mechanistic Interpretability & AI Alignment

The grokking phenomenon is not merely a curiosity about small toy models. It reveals something profound about how neural networks can harbour hidden structure — appearing to behave one way (memorisation) while internally building a very different capability (generalisation).

A model that appears safe during training may have internally built capabilities it is not yet deploying. Grokking shows that training loss and generalisation capability can be radically decoupled — and that decoupling is invisible from the outside.

Why Mechanistic Interpretability Matters for Safety

AI models can develop internal representations and circuits that are invisible to surface-level evaluation. Just as a grokked model hides its generalisation circuit during the memorisation phase, a misaligned model might conceal dangerous capabilities during training — only surfacing them under different prompting conditions or deployment settings.

This phenomenon — sometimes called instrumental convergence — suggests that undesirable internal goals may be preserved and even reinforced if they help the model complete its training objective, even if they never surface during evaluation.

🔬

Circuit-Level Analysis

By identifying specific circuits (like the Fourier circuit in this study), mechanistic interpretability allows us to verify that a model's capabilities are grounded in the right reasons, not spurious correlations.

✂️

Surgical Intervention

Once a problematic circuit is identified, it can be selectively ablated or modified — "brain surgery" on the model — without disturbing the rest of its capabilities.

🤖

Sparse Autoencoders (SAEs)

Automated interpretability tools like SAEs can extract human-readable features from high-dimensional activations, scaling circuit analysis to much larger models than can be hand-inspected.

The Road Ahead

The grokking story offers a rare gift: a setting where the ground truth algorithm is known (modular Fourier arithmetic), enabling us to fully validate our mechanistic analysis. The techniques developed here — Fourier decomposition of weights, progress measures, circuit identification — are the building blocks for interpretability of far larger and more consequential systems.

As AI systems become more capable, the gap between what they appear to be doing and what they are actually computing will only widen. Mechanistic interpretability is the toolkit that lets us close that gap — and grokking is one of its most instructive case studies.
